Referee Facing Rules

Keep the procedural stuff set in stone, expose your tables, let the players exploit their hard won knowledge.

Be fun.

The Die of Fate

Be a fan of the characters. Don't give them everything they ask for. When you need some serious 'Neutral Arbiter Inspiration' chuck out your DIE OF FATE !! It's a d6. Results of 1 are bad for the players, results of 6 are great for them. Adjust as the fates require.

Time and Turns

In Down-Time, time flows around as we require it to. We can pass of weeks at a time to accomplish tasks, or drill into moments for important conversations.

While hex-crawling, we will break the day down into Morning, Afternoon, Evening and Night chunks, where you can travel, forage, search and die with ease.

During Dungeon Crawls, we measure time in turns, which are 10 minute chunks of time.


During fights we measure time in rounds, which are 10 - 15 second chunks of chaos. You can move to an adjacent 'zone' and do something here there. If you are not doing anything but moving, you can move to a 'faraway zone'.
Actions fire off simultaneously each round, with initiative winners having a slight edge up on the losers.

An encounter takes up a 10 minute turn, with the remaining minutes used cleaning swords, checking equipment, looting bodies...

Adventure Day

Breaking up the day into three watches of 5 hours, and a night of 9 hours works great:

  • Morning: 6 7 8 9 10
  • Afternoon:11 12 1 2 3
  • Evening: 4 5 6 7 8
  • Night: 9 10 11 12 1 2 3 4 5

Random Encounters, Wandering Assholes and Fighting to the Death

I check for random encounters once a day while hex-crawling, and each night (2 or 3 times at night if the area is dangerous). While dungeon delving, I check every third turn (or every second turn in dangerous areas). If the characters are especially noisy with their actions, or if they players are taking a LONG time to choose a course of action I check again then too.

What checking for encounters means is that I roll a d6:

  • if the result is a 1 the characters face an encounter on the next ten minute turn.
  • If the result is a 2, I signpost the next encounter and when the next encounter check results in a 1, that is the encounter I use.


This mechanic is a time pressure to put on the players so they can't reroll lock-picking checks 200 times until they succeed at everything.
Random encounters also add variety and verisimilitude to your game. The key is that the players don't have to fight each little thing. Again...
An encounter does not mean instant battle !!! Far from it. This is probably the biggest feature that defines OSR play in my book.
For each encounter, roll both a reaction roll, and a range roll.

Notice that only 1 result on the list indicates an immediate fight. If it does come to blows, Most creatures WILL NOT fight to the death!.
Put yourselves in the trolls shoes. If these four assholes are obviously going to kill you, you will run away!
You can just make it happen, it's realistic, and realism is more fun. If you want a rule, the tried and true morale check is there for you.

  • Have the leader make a Charisma check.
    Failure means they want to bug out.
    • Good times to call for this check are when:
      • Half of the combatants have died.
      • The leader has lost Ability points.
      • The Solo Creature has lost Ability points.
      • Anytime after that.
  • In other Old School Systems

    • You would roll 2d6 and results over the creature's morale means they want to flee.

Hex-Crawling

All of these mechanics are referee facing. Keep a copy of your hex map (Mine is coloured pencil designating terrain types, with little numbered ink icons designating features [house 1; or cave 6]). Keep a page or two after your map keying the features.
If the PCs need a map, roughly draw out the features and locations the locals would know about. Half the fun of a hex-crawl is getting lost, and discovering the hidden shrine in the forest just outside of town.

Procedure

  1. Each Morning, roll to see if the party gets lost, or has an encounter in the day ahead.
  2. Roll to see how the weather has changed (I roll this ahead of time by a month or two)
  3. Determine how many • (Travel Points) the party can spend for the day.
  4. Describe the area around the characters, keep the focus zoomed out until an encounter or a feature is discovered.
    Listen to the player's intent for their turn.
    • Traveling: Spend • to move along the hexes as needed.
      • As each hex is entered, describe the area around the characters, and let them change course if desired.
      • When they enter a hex for the first time, determine if they stumble into the hex's feature.
    • Searching: Spend • to search in the current hex.
    • Hunting: Same procedure as Searching.
    • Camping?: Follow the camping procedure.
  5. Repeat until the day's time is spent.
  6. Nighttime: Roll 1 encounter check for each point of terrain cost (2 checks in Tough Terrain).
    Roll 1 less encounter check for each action the players take in fiction to hide themselves, but always roll one check at a minimum.
    Make sure the characters have Eaten, Drank and Slept to prevent Deprivation.

Careful: If traveling 'safely', lose one of the daily •'s, but:

  • Encounters are 1in6 less likely to occur.
  • Encounters occur at further ranges (2d6 +2).


Unsuitable Mounts: Riding a horse through tough terrain drops daily • points to 1.

Pushing: If 'pushing' the travel rate, gain an additional •, but:

  • Encounters are 1in6 more likely to occur.
  • Encounters occur at a closer range (2d6-4).
  • Pushing a donkey results in losing one • for the day.
  • Beasts of burden you push must make a Strength save at the end of the day's travel to prevent Deprivation.
    Failing a Strength save while already Deprived results in the animals death.

Lost? Encounter?

You roll two d6, one light and one dark, and compare them against the type of terrain you expect the party to travel in throughout the day.

Modifiers: Modify the x in 6 chance if:

  1. In a ranger's favoured biome, -1 to the lost chance, and any encounters occur at +4 range.
  2. Traveling Carefully, -1 to encounter chance, and any encounters occur at +2 range.

Lost: Rolling on or under the terrain cost on a d6 indicates the party will get their travel direction wrong at some point in the day. You don't need to directly inform them they are lost, but could hint at the fact.
At one point when they would change position to a new hex, roll a d6 and adjust their movement as follows:

  1. They have traveled in the completely wrong direction, and move backward one hex.
  2. They have circled back on themselves, and end in the same hex as they started.
  3. Same as 2.
  4. They have strayed off course, and moved to the hex to the RIGHT on their intended direction.
  5. They have strayed off course, and moved to the hex to the LEFT of their intended direction.
  6. Luckily they have blindly wandered into the hex they intended to travel to.

Encounter: Rolling on or under the terrain cost on a d6 indicates the party will face an encounter at some point in the adventuring day. You can choose when this happens, or roll a d12 and let that dictate the hour it occurs.
Be sure to roll for Range and Reaction

Features - Seeing, Searching, Hunting

The first time the characters enter a new hex, roll an appropriate 'search' die for that terrain type.
Rolling the maximum result means the party has stumbled across the hex's main feature. Roll only on the first time entering, after that, the players have to search.

When the players actively Search, or Hunt follow this procedure:

  • Spend the terrain type's • cost.
  • Secretly roll the terrain types search die.
    • Rolling a die's maximum results in the players finding the hex's feature, and any secret locations.
    • Rolling a die's maximum -1 results in them only finding the feature.
    • Maximum when hunting means they hunted "d8 rations worth of meat", and a creatures' den.
    • Maximum -1 means just the d8 rations of meat.
  • Roll another Encounter Check.


Modify results of the search die by:

  • +1 in a ranger's favoured terrain.
  • -1 for shitty weather

Camping

The characters need to eat, drink and sleep for at least 6 hours each day or be Deprived. Characters are also Deprived if they are sleeping outside, exposed to the elements. Each occurance of becoming Deprived 'stacks' and becomes Exhaustion.

If the players look for an advantageous place to camp or set a fire, roll a Die of Fate.
See also: Sleeping Inside.
The dangers of sleeping outside can be mitigated with Camping Gear. One character with Camping Gear covers the party.

  • If you spend the night in a tent, make a Charisma Save.
    • Success means you can heal 1 point of Ability loss.

You can get up to a -4 bonus to the Charisma Save by:

  • Eating a hot, cooked meal before bed: -2
    • Cooking requires a fire, and a Mess Kit.
  • Enjoying the company of your friends: -2
    • One character can tell a story or sing a song, then succeed on a Charisma Save.

Fire

Each character gathering wood will find 1d6 hours of wood by spending 20 minutes foraging.
That's in a forest, reduce the d6 as appropriate.

Starting a fire requires either a tinderbox, or 30 minutes and a Dex save.
If conditions are bad, put an xin6 chance on the tinderbox or a penalty on the Dex save.


It should always be a trade-off to have a fire or not. Here are some thoughts:

  • Fire provides vision for watches (otherwise, Charisma saves to see approaching threats are made at +4).
  • Fires can be seen from afar (When making encounter checks at night, adjust the xin6 chance by -2)...
    ...unless the players have been crafty and hid the fire away somehow...
  • If you are in freezing temperatures, a fire is REQUIRED.
    • If the characters sleep in the freezing elements without a fire:
      • They are going to lose d8 Dexterity, and
      • they have to pass a Dexterity save in the morning to not be frozen to death.
      • If they survive, they are Deprived.

Sailing

Got a ship? They can travel 20 six mile hexes in a day (1.2hours per) or 3 thirty six mile hexes (8 hr per)

xin6 chance for encounters or being lost is based on weather: Calm 1in6, Iffy 2in6, Stormy 3in6.

Give each character a job, and on days that there are no encounters, choose a player and make up some minor problem they have with the job, a slight drama to deal with or ask them a question re: what they see.

Dungeon Crawling

  1. Describe the surroundings, ask what they are doing.
    • Don't let one player dominate, ask "And what are You doing while He does that?".
  2. Each action takes a 10 minute turn. In it you can:
    • Search a Room.
    • Take a break to refresh hp.
    • Cover about 120' at a normal pace.
    • check an area for traps.
    • Etc.
      • No need for Saves unless there is time pressure.
  3. Restate the character's surroundings and ask again what they are up to now.


Make an encounter check:

  • Every third turn (every second in dangerous areas).
  • When the characters do something noisy.
  • When the Players can't seem to make up their mind for a while.



Note: not all dungeons are underground. Treat point crawls, city crawls and forest crawls in the same manner.

Sleeping in the Dungeon

It's a bad idea, don't let them do it. Here is the choice.
Sleeping in the dungeon prompts three encounter checks through the night. An encounter happens on 2in6 in regular dungeons, or 3in6 in dangerous areas.
If fictional positioning allows you to reduce them, okay, but not below 2 checks and an encounter in 2in6.

When the inevitable happens, and they have an encounter though the night, hit them with the whole bus, and ensure they go under the wheels.
The dungeon is not safe to sleep in. Think of the worst thing that can happen here, and make it worse than that.
Try this helpful list of examples:

  • Enemies barricade or bar the door the PCs are behind from the outside.
  • Enemies lure or release some vicious beast on the PCs.
  • If the door is locked, they tie the beast to the door frame on the other side.
  • Set up an ambush with crossbows.
  • Setup an ambush with crossbows, having pulled tables and cabinets around to hide behind.
  • The enemies grabbed all the treasure they knew about, and just left the dungeon.
  • An ambush when the PCs emerge with buckets of oil and flaming arrows.
  • Set a new trap in a previously cleared area. Re-activate disarmed ones.
  • Fireball Spell!
  • Call in a HUGE amount of reinforcements.
  • Flood the chamber the PCs are sleeping in. Combine with the barricaded door!
  • Douse the PCs torches. Nets and clubs!
  • Collapse the ceiling on the PCs
  • Lure the PCs somewhere very bad.
  • Send in a 'trader'. Shoot the PCs from the dark when they pull their gear out.
  • Enemies lead a bunch of undead to the PCs.
